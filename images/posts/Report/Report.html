Primer on Zymology using Honey

Jonathan Myers, Kyle Carretto

Rochester Institute of Technology

Abstract

In this paper we will discuss a new and trending subject in zymology which is the brewing of honey to create an alcoholic beverage known as mead.  Mead is made by fermenting honey with water.  Mead is oftentimes flavored with fruits and spices but it's generally higher in alcohol than beer and is much more similar to wine.  Additionally mead is produced in a variety of sweetness levels and can be still or sparkling.  We will specifically cover the process we used, techniques that we discovered, mistakes that we made and advice for other homebrewers.  

                We began our adventure in homebrewing by first doing research on the materials that were required.  Our initial searches led us to simply buying a kit that is used for most small homebrew setups.  This kit consisted of a 1.5 gallon fermentation bucket, a 1 gallon carboy, a beer thief (pump and tube for moving the mead between containers), a three piece airlock, campton tables, and yeast nutrient.  For $10 more we added a thermometer but opted out of the large spoon that would fit in the carboy.    

 

Figure 1: Materials

These materials were not a horrible combination since you can successfully brew with these items but it is key to know exactly what materials you need and what they are used for.  The large spoon appeared to be much more useful than anticipated, having a hydrometer to take the initial and final gravity is almost essential, and having a vessel to take the gravity check in is also important.  After acquiring the initial kit we set out to find what honey would be have the best value in our area.  This is key as the type of honey you use largely dictates the notes your mead will emit once fermented.  We chose to use mesquite honey which can be purchased in large quantities from Trader Joes.  After some conversation with other mazers we have decided that it might be a better idea to purchase honey from local apiaries at nearby farmer markets but this is purely a personal preference.  Finally we sought the perfect yeast to produce the type of mead that we desired and what would work best in our small operation.  We chose to use Lelvin 71b-1122 which is “a rapid starting and constant fermenting semi-dry white wine yeast that will enhance fruit flavors and add fruity esters”. (Lelvin)  Typically this yeast is used in melomels, or fruit meads, as it flourishes at handling the fermentation of fruit.  It also has an alcohol tolerance of 14% although we did not reach this in our first attempt.  

Our process for making the mead was standard among mazers/methiers, 3 pounds of honey for every gallon of water.  After watching some videos other homebrewers have posted we chose to use the glass carboy as our primary fermenter.  This was suboptimal as we did not take in consideration that we could not fit 3 pounds of honey and 1 gallon of water in a 1 gallon container.  This resulted in an incredibly sweet concoction which the yeast could not fully ferment.  In future endeavours we chose to instead use the over sized fermentation bucket which allowed for the proper amount of space for the ingredients.  It is also important to note that Rochester tap water has a large amount of chlorine which is not optimal for the must.  So we purchased a large tank of mineral water to counter this problem.  Next we ensured that everything is sanitized thoroughly which is arguably the most important practice to use when brewing anything.  We chose to use a diluted bleach solution however this has a lot of downsides such as chlorine getting into the must and off flavors being produced.  In future endeavours we chose to use StarSan as it is an effective cleaning solution that can be air dried.  It will also turn into food for the yeast after a certain period of time promoting a stronger fermentation.  The process for filling the carboy consisted of draining the honey into the carboy and then mixing water to fill the vessel.

Figure 2: Adding Water to the Honey

Next we began hydrating the yeast which some brewers believe to be essential while others note that they are too lazy to use this practice.  After some research we saw a trend that suggested that hydrating the yeast will encourage a much stronger fermentation and prove to be more effective however we did not test this ourselves.  This process consisted of boiling some of the mineral water and putting it into a sanitized container while allowing it to cool to the ideal starting temperature for the yeast (104-109 degrees fahrenheit).  Once this temperature was reached we dumped the packet of yeast into the water and immediately stirred it resulting in a very explosive production of foam.  This was also suboptimal and the correct technique is to slowly sift the yeast onto the water using a spoon.  After waiting about 15 minutes you may stir the yeast and pitch it into the must.  It is important to note that the temperature of the hydrated yeast and the must should be within 10 degrees fahrenheit of each other.  If they are not, you run into the problem of shocking the yeast and causing a poor fermentation.  

Figure 3: Yeast Hydration Prior to Stirring

After this we measured out 1 tsp of yeast nutrient for every gallon of mead and poured it into the carboy.  Finally we applied the airlock, breaking it in the process, and put the mead in a dark location to begin fermenting.  We were expecting the airlock to begin bubbling after 24 to 48 hours but this did not occur.  After about three days we came to the conclusion that we did something wrong.  We invested in a new “S” shaped airlock and some new yeast since our initial intuition was that we over sanitized and killed our yeast.  This turned out to be incorrect as we learned that the yeast requires initial aeration to properly ferment.  We chose to then violently stir the carboy with the thermometer as well as rotating the mead in the carboy.  Then we applied the airlock and let it sit where after a few hours we discovered that we were producing bubbles in the airlock.  

Figure 4: Bubble Production

After exactly 3 weeks the bubbles began to halter and eventually the fermentation ceased all together.  This was alarming as the average fermentation typically takes 4-6 weeks.  We purchased a hydrometer and took what ended up being the final gravity which read 1.07 which ment we produced what we believe to be approximately 10% alcohol.  This was not too concerning as we enjoy sweeter wines but we are unsure if this is an accurate reading as we did not take an initial gravity and we used a larger honey to water ratio.  At this point we decided to rack the mead since we read that 71b tends to produce flavors of burnt rubber if autolysis sets in (Autolysis occurs when the mead sits on the lees, or dead yeast, for too long).  This lead us to discovering another important problem: some of the wine will be left in the original carboy when you rack into a new carboy.  This produced a small but noticeable amount of head space which means that oxygen can set in and create another plethora of off flavors.  At this point we came to the realization that fermenting a larger amount of mead in the bucket fermenter is much more optimal.  We did however discover that most people solve this problem by sanitizing marbles and adding them to the must to reduce the headspace.  However we discovered this technique after we chose to add a honey-water mixture to the mead.

Figure 5: Racking

After a few weeks we decided to rack again and finally taste/smell the mead.  The initial aromas that the mead produced smelled a lot like glue which was displeasing but the taste was not nearly as bad as we expected.  We had produced a very sweet mead and after aging for sometime expect the glue aromas to wear off and more subtle flavors to emerge.

        While our initial operation had some problems, we discovered a lot of solutions to these problems and decided to upscale the operation to a 6 gallon batch using the exact same ingredients and ratios.  During this run we plan on applying apricots to the secondary fermentation to produce an apricot melomel.  There are many different opinions about when to add fruit to the mead but we decided that the secondary would be the least risky since fruit tends to affect fermentation times drastically.  

 

Figure 6: Operation B

During this attempt we are using the 6.5 gallon fermenting bucket, StarStan sanitizer, a pestle and mortar to grind the Campton tablets, and a hydrometer to take an initial gravity check.  This time we decided to use the Campton tablets properly and ground them up before pitching them into the must.  Then we let the must sit for 24 hours before pitching the yeast to ensure all contamination would be eliminated.  We took an initial gravity before pitching the yeast which read 1.1.  This is the standard gravity for the quantity and ratio of ingredients that we are using.  After pitching the yeast we started experiencing bubbling in the airlock after merly 12 hours which is a much better than before.  Ideally this fermentation will finish on 5/12/2018 where we will inspect what we produced.
